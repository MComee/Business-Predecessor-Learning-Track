# Counterexample and Falsification Protocol

## Purpose

This protocol prevents attractive lessons from becoming patterns or principles without testing.

The repository studies business history. It must preserve cases where a lesson fails, reverses, becomes illegal or unethical today, or depends on conditions that no longer apply.

## Required Use

Apply this protocol to:

- major claims;
- accepted lessons;
- candidate patterns;
- accepted patterns;
- strong patterns;
- principles.

## Counterexample Requirements

Every accepted pattern and principle should document:

- at least one considered counterexample, when known;
- whether the counterexample weakens, limits, or disproves the pattern;
- whether the counterexample comes from a different industry, era, geography, capital structure, or regulatory environment;
- why the pattern still holds, if it remains accepted.

## Falsification Questions

Before promotion, ask:

1. What evidence would disprove this?
2. What known target appears to contradict this?
3. Does the lesson fail under different capital structures?
4. Does the lesson fail in different regulatory environments?
5. Does the lesson fail outside founder-led companies?
6. Does the lesson depend on obsolete technology, law, financing, labor conditions, or ethics?
7. Does the lesson describe a historical tactic that should not be copied today?

## Status Outcomes

After counterexample review, a knowledge object may remain:

- `candidate`
- `accepted`
- `disputed`
- `retired`

Patterns may use:

- `candidate-pattern`
- `accepted-pattern`
- `strong-pattern`
- `disputed-pattern`
- `retired-pattern`

Principles may use:

- `candidate-principle`
- `accepted-principle`
- `disputed-principle`
- `retired-principle`

## Preservation Rule

Do not delete falsified or weakened ideas merely to keep the repository clean.

Preserve why they failed so future work does not rediscover the same mistake.
